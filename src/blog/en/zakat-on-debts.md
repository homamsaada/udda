## Part 1: Zakat on Money You've Lent

If you've loaned someone money, it is still legally yours. But do you pay Zakat on it? It depends on the type of debt:

### Collectible Debt (Likely to Be Repaid)

If the borrower is able to pay and you expect repayment, Zakat is due. Scholars differ on the timing:

- **Majority view**: Pay Zakat on it annually along with your other wealth, as if it were in your possession.
- **Alternative view**: Pay Zakat on it when recovered, covering all past years.
- **Third view**: Pay Zakat once upon recovery, for one year only.

**The safest approach**: Include it in your annual Zakat calculation, which is what the Zakat calculator does automatically when you enter "money lent" as an asset.

### Uncollectible Debt (Unlikely to Be Repaid)

If the borrower is unable to pay or is unlikely to repay, no Zakat is due until you actually recover the money. When recovered, most scholars say you pay Zakat for one year only.

## Part 2: Can I Deduct Debts I Owe?

If you owe money to others, the question is whether to subtract it from your Zakatable wealth.

### Currently Due Debts

Installments actually due now — this month's loan payment, bills payable — are deducted from your Zakatable wealth. This is agreed upon by most scholars.

### Deferred Debts (Long-Term)

For long-term debts like remaining mortgage or car loan installments over several years, scholars differ:

- **One view**: Deduct the entire remaining debt.
- **Preferred contemporary view**: Deduct only the current year's installment, not all future payments. If you deducted a 20-year mortgage entirely, you would never pay Zakat, which contradicts its purpose.

## Comprehensive Practical Example

Sarah has:

- Bank balance: $20,000
- Loaned to her sister: $4,000 (sister can repay)
- Loaned to a neighbor: $1,500 (neighbor is in financial hardship)

She owes:

- Car payment due this month: $400
- Remaining car payments: $8,000 (over 2 years)
- Utility bill due: $150

**Calculating Zakatable wealth:**

Counted assets: $20,000 (bank) + $4,000 (sister's collectible debt) = $24,000

Note: The $1,500 from the neighbor is excluded as uncollectible debt.

Deductions: $400 (current car payment) + $150 (bill) = $550

Note: Remaining car payments ($8,000) are not deducted as they are deferred.

Zakatable amount: $24,000 - $550 = **$23,450**

**Zakat**: $23,450 × 2.5% = **$586.25**

## Quick Summary

| Situation | Ruling |
|-----------|--------|
| Money lent to someone able to repay | Pay Zakat on it annually |
| Money lent to someone unable to repay | No Zakat until recovered |
| Debt due now (current installment) | Deduct from Zakatable wealth |
| Deferred debt (future installments) | Do not deduct (only deduct this year's portion) |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**I lent a friend money and forgot about it — is Zakat due?**
If you remember and recover it, pay Zakat for one year. If you completely forgot and never counted it among your assets, some scholars say nothing is owed for past years.

**I have credit card debt — can I deduct it?**
Deduct only the amount currently due (this month's statement), not your total credit limit.

**I bought property on installments — can I deduct the full amount?**
Deduct only the current year's installments, not the entire remaining balance.
