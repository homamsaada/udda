## The Ruling: Yes, Zakat Is Due

**The rule is clear**: Any cash that reaches the Nisab and has been held for one lunar year is subject to Zakat — regardless of what you intend to use it for.

Whether saving for a house, car, wedding, or travel — as long as the money is in your account, has reached the Nisab, and a year has passed, Zakat is obligatory.

### Why?

Because the money is still fully yours. Intention doesn't remove it from your ownership. This is different from the house you live in or the car you drive. For details on Zakat on property itself, see [Zakat on Real Estate](/en/blog/zakat-on-real-estate.html).

## Practical Examples

**Saving for a house**: $80,000 saved for over a year → Zakat = $80,000 × 2.5% = **$2,000**

**Saving for a wedding**: $15,000 saved for a year → Zakat = $15,000 × 2.5% = **$375**

**Below Nisab**: $800 saved → **No Zakat** (below Nisab)

For the full Nisab guide, see [comprehensive Zakat calculation guide](/en/blog/how-to-calculate-zakat.html).

## Do I Combine Savings with Other Money?

Yes. **All your cash is combined** when calculating Zakat. You cannot separate "house money" from "expense money."

## Will Paying Zakat Delay My Goal?

Zakat is only 2.5%. On $80,000 that's $2,000. And you can subtract due debts — see [Zakat on Debts](/en/blog/zakat-on-debts.html).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Isn't saving for marriage a basic need?**
No. Exempt needs are what you're currently using. Cash saved hasn't been spent yet.

**I have a dedicated savings account — is the ruling different?**
No difference.

**I'm saving in an investment fund — how do I calculate?**
Pay Zakat on the fund's market value on your Zakat date. See [Zakat on Stocks and Funds](/en/blog/zakat-on-stocks.html).
