Many heirs are surprised to learn that a sibling receives nothing from the estate despite being a close relative. The reason is the system of "Hajb" (blocking) in Islamic inheritance — one of the most impactful rules in determining who gets what. This article explains when siblings are blocked and by whom.

## What Is Hajb (Blocking)?

Hajb means that one heir's presence prevents another from inheriting entirely or reduces their share. There are two types:

- **Total blocking (Hajb Hirman)**: The heir is completely excluded from inheritance.
- **Partial blocking (Hajb Nuqsan)**: The heir still inherits but receives a reduced share.

## Who Blocks Siblings Entirely?

### The Father Blocks All Siblings

This is the fundamental rule agreed upon by all scholars: **the father blocks all brothers and sisters** of every type (full, paternal, and maternal). If the father is alive, no sibling inherits anything.

### Sons and Grandsons Block Siblings

A male descendant (son or grandson however far) blocks full and paternal siblings. Maternal siblings are blocked by any descendant — male or female.

### The Grandfather — Does He Block Siblings?

This is a famous area of disagreement:

- **Hanafi school**: The grandfather blocks siblings just like the father.
- **Majority (Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali)**: The grandfather does not block full or paternal siblings — he inherits alongside them through a sharing arrangement.

This is one of the most significant differences between schools, which is why the inheritance calculator gives different results when selecting the Hanafi school versus others.

## Complete Blocking Table for Siblings

| Blocked Heir | Blocked By |
|-------------|-----------|
| Full brother | Father, son, grandson |
| Full sister | Father, son, grandson (except when with daughter — becomes residuary) |
| Paternal brother | Father, son, grandson, full brother |
| Paternal sister | Father, son, grandson, full brother, two full sisters (except with daughter) |
| Maternal brother | Father, grandfather, any descendant (male or female) |
| Maternal sister | Same as maternal brother |

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Father Blocks Siblings

A woman dies leaving: husband, father, mother, full brother.

- Husband: 1/2
- Father: 1/6 + residuary
- Mother: 1/3 or 1/6
- **Full brother: blocked by the father — inherits nothing**

### Example 2: Son Blocks Siblings

A man dies leaving: wife, son, full brother.

- Wife: 1/8
- Son: residuary
- **Full brother: blocked by the son — inherits nothing**

### Example 3: Sister with Daughter (Special Case)

A man dies leaving: daughter, full sister.

- Daughter: 1/2
- **Full sister: takes the remainder as residuary (becomes residuary with the daughter)**

This is a special case: a full sister is normally blocked by a son, but when there's a daughter without a son, the sister becomes a residuary heir.

## Partial Blocking — When Shares Are Reduced

Not all blocking means exclusion. Sometimes the share is merely reduced:

- **Mother**: Moves from 1/3 to 1/6 when descendants or multiple siblings exist.
- **Husband**: Moves from 1/2 to 1/4 when descendants exist.
- **Wife**: Moves from 1/4 to 1/8 when descendants exist.
- **Daughter**: Moves from 1/2 to a portion of 2/3 when another daughter exists.

## Why Does the Blocking System Exist?

Blocking isn't injustice — it's a fair system built on clear wisdom: the closer relative has greater claim to the deceased's wealth. A son is closer than a brother, a father is closer than a grandfather, and a full brother is closer than a paternal brother. The closer relative also bears greater responsibility toward the deceased's family — the son will support his mother and younger siblings, which is why he receives the larger share while more distant relatives are blocked. This logic repeats in every blocking rule, and understanding it helps you make sense of results that may seem surprising at first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My brother died while his father is alive — do my brother's children inherit?**
No. The father blocks siblings, and therefore the siblings' children are also blocked since they are more distant.

**I'm a full sister and my brother died — do I inherit if he has children?**
If he has a son, you don't inherit (blocked). But if he left only a daughter with no son, you inherit the remainder as a residuary heir.

**Are maternal siblings blocked by a daughter?**
Yes. Any descendant — male or female — blocks maternal siblings.
