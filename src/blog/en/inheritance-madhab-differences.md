Most inheritance rules are agreed upon across the four schools of Islamic jurisprudence because they are based on explicit Quranic texts. However, there are important cases where the division differs significantly depending on which school is followed. This article highlights the key disagreements in a clear, practical way.

## 1. Grandfather with Siblings

This is the most famous dispute in inheritance and has the greatest impact on outcomes.

**Hanafi school**: The grandfather blocks siblings completely, just like the father. If the grandfather exists, no sibling inherits.

**Majority (Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali)**: The grandfather does not block full or paternal siblings. Instead, he inherits alongside them through a sharing arrangement — he takes the best of three options: sharing as if he were a brother, one-third of the remainder, or one-sixth of the estate.

### Example

A man dies leaving: grandfather and two full brothers.

| School | Grandfather | Each Brother |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Hanafi | Entire estate | Nothing (blocked) |
| Majority | 1/3 | 1/3 |

A massive difference — brothers receive two-thirds under the majority and nothing under the Hanafi school.

## 2. Returning Remainder to Spouses (Radd)

When shares are distributed and a remainder exists with no residuary heir, the remainder is returned to fixed-share heirs. But does this include the spouse?

**Hanafi, Maliki, and Shafi'i**: No return to the spouse. The remainder goes to distant kindred or the state treasury.

**Hanbali**: If no other heir exists, the remainder is returned to the spouse.

### Example

A woman dies leaving: only her husband (no other heirs).

| School | Husband's Share |
|--------|----------------|
| Majority | 1/2 only (remainder to state) |
| Hanbali | Entire estate (1/2 by right + remainder by return) |

## 3. The Shared Case (Mushtaraka / Himariyya)

A rare but famous case: a woman dies leaving husband, mother, maternal siblings, and full siblings.

**The problem**: Maternal siblings take 1/3 by fixed share. Full siblings are residuary heirs who take the remainder. But after the husband's 1/2, mother's 1/6, and maternal siblings' 1/3, nothing remains for full siblings.

**Hanafi and Hanbali**: Full siblings receive nothing — the rules are applied as-is.

**Maliki and Shafi'i**: Full siblings share the 1/3 with maternal siblings, since they share the same mother. This is why it's called "the shared case."

### Numerical Example

A woman dies leaving: husband, mother, two maternal brothers, two full brothers.

| School | Husband | Mother | Maternal Brothers | Full Brothers |
|--------|---------|--------|------------------|--------------|
| Hanafi/Hanbali | 1/2 | 1/6 | 1/3 | Nothing |
| Maliki/Shafi'i | 1/2 | 1/6 | Share 1/3 | Share the 1/3 with maternal brothers |

## 4. The Umariyyatan (Two Omari Cases)

Two famous cases named after Umar ibn al-Khattab who adjudicated them:

**Case 1**: Woman dies leaving husband, father, and mother.
**Case 2**: Man dies leaving wife, father, and mother.

**The dispute**: Does the mother take 1/3 of the entire estate or 1/3 of the remainder after the spouse's share?

**Majority**: The mother takes 1/3 of the remainder (following Umar's ruling).
**Ibn Abbas and followers**: The mother takes 1/3 of the entire estate.

## 5. Radd in the Shafi'i School

**Shafi'is** do not apply Radd at all. If a remainder exists after fixed shares and there's no residuary heir, the remainder goes to the state treasury — not back to fixed-share heirs.

**Other schools**: Return the remainder to fixed-share heirs (excluding spouses in the majority view).

In practice, where no functioning state treasury exists, Radd is applied even in Shafi'i-majority countries.

## Summary of Key Differences

| Issue | Hanafi | Maliki | Shafi'i | Hanbali |
|-------|--------|--------|---------|---------|
| Grandfather with siblings | Blocks them | Shares with them | Shares with them | Shares with them |
| Radd to spouses | No | No | No | Yes (if no one else) |
| Shared case | No sharing | Sharing | Sharing | No sharing |
| Radd in general | Yes | Yes | No (to state) | Yes |

## How to Choose a School?

Typically, you follow the school applied in your country's Sharia courts. Saudi Arabia follows the Hanbali school, Egypt follows the Hanafi with legal modifications, Morocco and Tunisia follow the Maliki, and so on.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does disagreement mean one school is wrong?**
No. All four schools are valid scholarly interpretations based on Islamic evidence. Disagreement in understanding and weighing evidence has existed since the time of the Companions.

**What if heirs disagree on which school to follow?**
The matter is referred to the Sharia court, which rules according to the country's adopted school.

**Can I follow one school for inheritance and another for worship?**
Yes, many scholars permit this, especially in inheritance where the country's legal system typically determines which school applies.
