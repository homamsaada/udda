With the growth of stock market investing and investment funds, "how do I pay Zakat on my shares?" has become one of the most common questions. The answer depends primarily on your intention when purchasing: are you a long-term investor or an active trader? This article explains the difference and the calculation method for each.

## Case 1: Active Trader (Buying and Selling for Profit)

If you buy stocks with the intention of selling them for profit from price differences, your shares are considered **trade goods** — just like a merchant buying merchandise to resell.

**Calculation method:**

On your Zakat date, take the current market value of all your shares (not the purchase price), and pay 2.5% of the total.

### Example

Khalid holds a trading portfolio:

- 500 shares of Company A at $12/share = $6,000
- 200 shares of Company B at $22/share = $4,400
- Cash in brokerage account: $1,500

Total market value: $6,000 + $4,400 + $1,500 = **$11,900**

**Zakat**: $11,900 × 2.5% = **$297.50**

## Case 2: Long-Term Investor (Holding for Dividends)

If you buy stocks to hold and benefit from distributed dividends without intending to sell, your shares are not trade goods.

**Calculation method:**

You pay Zakat only on the dividends you actually received, if they reach the Nisab and a year has passed. You do not pay Zakat on the share value itself.

### Example

Sarah holds stocks as a long-term investment. During the year, she received $2,000 in dividends, which she added to her bank balance.

These dividends are added to her other cash assets in her annual Zakat calculation. If her total (bank balance + dividends + savings) reaches the Nisab and a year has passed, she pays 2.5% on the total.

## Case 3: Investment Funds

Investment funds follow the same logic:

- **Trading funds and ETFs**: If you actively buy and sell units, pay Zakat on the full market value.
- **Income/distribution funds**: If you hold for dividends only, pay Zakat on distributed profits.
- **REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)**: Treated based on your intention — trading or long-term income.

## What If My Intentions Are Mixed?

Some people hold shares with different intentions — some for trading, others for long-term investment. In this case:

- Classify each holding based on your original intention at purchase.
- Trading stocks: Pay Zakat on market value.
- Investment stocks: Pay Zakat on dividends only.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My stocks are at a loss — is Zakat still due?**
If you are a trader, you pay Zakat on the current market value even if it's below your purchase price. Zakat is on what you own now, not what you paid.

**Do I pay Zakat on shares I can't sell (lock-up period)?**
Yes, because they are still your property with calculable value. A temporary restriction does not cancel the Zakat obligation.

**How do I find the market value on my Zakat date?**
Open your brokerage app on your Zakat date and check the total "market value" of your portfolio. That's the number you need.
