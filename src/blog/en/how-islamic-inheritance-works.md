The Islamic science of inheritance (Ilm al-Faraid) is one of the most precise areas of Islamic law. Allah Himself prescribed the distribution of estates in the Quran, leaving little room for human discretion. Yet many Muslims find it complex due to its numerous rules. This article provides a simplified overview to help you understand the fundamentals.

## What Happens Before Distribution

Before dividing the estate among heirs, four things are taken out in order:

1. **Funeral expenses**: Washing, shrouding, and burial costs.
2. **Debt repayment**: All debts owed by the deceased are settled first.
3. **Executing the will**: If the deceased left a bequest for a non-heir, it is honored up to one-third of the estate.
4. **Distribution**: The remainder is divided among heirs according to Islamic rules.

To learn how debts affect Zakat as well, see [Zakat on Debts](/en/blog/zakat-on-debts.html).

## Who Inherits? Categories of Heirs

Heirs fall into three categories:

### Fixed-Share Heirs (Ashab al-Furud)

These heirs receive specific fractions prescribed in the Quran and Sunnah. The six fixed shares are: one-half (1/2), one-quarter (1/4), one-eighth (1/8), two-thirds (2/3), one-third (1/3), and one-sixth (1/6).

Key fixed-share heirs include: husband, wife, father, mother, daughter, son's daughter, full sister, paternal sister, maternal brother, maternal sister, grandfather, and grandmother.

### Residuary Heirs (Asabat)

These heirs receive whatever remains after fixed shares are distributed. If nothing remains, they receive nothing. Priority order: son, then grandson, then father, then grandfather, then full brother, then paternal brother.

### Distant Kindred (Dhawu al-Arham)

Relatives who inherit neither by fixed share nor as residuary heirs (such as daughter's children, maternal uncle, or paternal aunt). They inherit only when no fixed-share or residuary heirs exist, according to some schools.

## Examples of Fixed Shares

| Heir | Share | Condition |
|------|-------|-----------|
| Husband | 1/2 | Wife has no children or grandchildren |
| Husband | 1/4 | Wife has children or grandchildren |
| Wife | 1/4 | Husband has no children or grandchildren |
| Wife | 1/8 | Husband has children or grandchildren |
| One daughter | 1/2 | No son present |
| Two+ daughters | 2/3 | No son present |
| Mother | 1/3 | No children/grandchildren and fewer than 2 siblings |
| Mother | 1/6 | Children/grandchildren exist or 2+ siblings |
| Father | 1/6 | Male descendant exists |

## What Is Blocking (Hajb)?

Blocking means one heir prevents another from inheriting entirely or reduces their share. Examples:

- **A son blocks a grandson** completely (total blocking).
- **A child reduces the mother's share** from 1/3 to 1/6 (partial blocking).
- **The father blocks all siblings** from inheriting.

## What Is Awl (Proportional Reduction)?

Awl occurs when the total fixed shares exceed the estate — meaning there isn't enough to give everyone their full share. In this case, all shares are reduced proportionally.

### Awl Example

A man dies leaving: a wife, two full sisters, and a mother.

- Wife: 1/4 (3/12)
- Two sisters: 2/3 (8/12)
- Mother: 1/6 (2/12)
- Total: 3 + 8 + 2 = 13 out of 12 ← **Awl applies**

The estate is divided by 13 instead of 12, and each heir receives a proportionally reduced share.

## What Is Radd (Return)?

Radd is the opposite of Awl — it occurs when shares are distributed but a remainder exists with no residuary heir to take it. The remainder is returned to fixed-share heirs in proportion to their shares (excluding spouses according to the majority).

## Do Schools of Jurisprudence Differ?

Yes, in certain cases the four schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) reach different conclusions. Key differences include:

- **Radd to spouses**: Hanbalis return the remainder to the spouse when no other heir exists; the majority do not.
- **Grandfather with siblings**: Does the grandfather block siblings like the father does? Hanafis say yes; the majority say no.
- **The shared case (Mushtaraka)**: A special scenario where scholars disagree.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does a non-Muslim inherit from a Muslim?**
No. A Muslim does not inherit from a non-Muslim and vice versa, by scholarly consensus.

**Does a killer inherit?**
No. Anyone who intentionally kills their benefactor is excluded from inheritance.

**Can an heir be disinherited?**
No. Inheritance shares are prescribed by Allah and cannot be altered or denied by anyone.

**What if heirs refuse to divide the estate?**
The estate becomes the heirs' property immediately upon death. Delaying division without everyone's consent is impermissible, and each heir has the right to demand their share.
