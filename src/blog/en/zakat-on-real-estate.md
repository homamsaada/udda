Many Muslims own property — houses, apartments, and land — and wonder: is Zakat due on them? The answer depends entirely on your intention. The same property may or may not be subject to Zakat depending on its purpose. This article covers the different scenarios with practical examples.

## Case 1: Personal Residence — No Zakat

The house you live in with your family is **not subject to Zakat** regardless of its value. It is a basic necessity like food and clothing. The same applies to a vacation home used personally.

## Case 2: Property for Sale (Trade Asset) — Zakat Is Due

If you purchased property with the intention of selling it for profit — land, an apartment, or a villa — it is a **trade asset** and Zakat is obligatory.

**Calculation**: Estimate the current market value on your Zakat date and pay 2.5%.

### Example

Ahmad bought land for $60,000 intending to sell when the price rises. After one year, the market value is $75,000.

**Zakat**: $75,000 × 2.5% = **$1,875**

Zakat is based on the current market value, not the purchase price.

**Important note**: If you bought land without a clear intention, most scholars hold that no Zakat is due until you firmly intend to sell. Intention is a key condition.

## Case 3: Rental Property — Zakat on Rent Only

If you own an apartment or building and rent it out for monthly or annual income, the property itself is **not subject to Zakat**. However, the rental income you collect is treated like any cash:

- If it reaches the Nisab and a year passes, Zakat is due at 2.5%.
- It is added to your other cash assets in your annual calculation.

### Example

Sarah owns an apartment rented at $800/month, collecting $9,600 annually. She spends $3,200 on maintenance, leaving $6,400 added to her savings.

This $6,400 joins her bank balance in her Zakat calculation. If her total cash reaches the Nisab and a year has passed, she pays 2.5%.

## Case 4: Real Estate Developer — Zakat Is Due

If you buy land, build on it, and sell units, you are a property trader. You pay Zakat on the value of properties held for sale + raw materials + cash in the project.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**I bought land for personal use then decided to sell — when does the Hawl start?**
The Hawl begins from the day you decided to sell (intention changed), not the purchase date.

**I own a building with my personal apartment and rented units — how do I calculate?**
Your personal apartment is exempt. For rented units, pay Zakat only on saved rental income.

**I've held land for years waiting for the price to rise — is Zakat due?**
If your intention is to sell for profit, yes — Zakat is due annually on the market value. If your intention is unclear, the safer approach is to pay.
