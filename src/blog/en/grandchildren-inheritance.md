One of the most emotionally charged questions in Islamic inheritance: a father dies, then later his father (the grandfather) dies — do the grandchildren inherit from their grandfather in place of their deceased father? The answer involves important distinctions between classical jurisprudence and modern family law. This article covers all aspects.

## The Classical Jurisprudence Rule

In Islamic law, inheritance applies only to the living. If a son dies before his father, when the father later dies, the deceased son inherits nothing (being dead), so nothing passes through him to his children (the grandchildren).

But can grandchildren inherit directly from their grandparent? This depends on their type and who else survives:

### Son's Sons (Grandsons through Sons)

A son's son can inherit from his grandfather in many cases:

- **If the deceased has no living son**: The grandson takes the son's place as a residuary heir.
- **If the deceased has a living son**: The grandson is **blocked** by the living son and inherits nothing.

### Son's Daughters

A son's daughter inherits in specific cases:

- If the deceased has no daughters or sons: She inherits 1/2 like a daughter.
- If the deceased has one daughter: She inherits 1/6 to complete 2/3.
- If the deceased has a living son: She is **blocked**.
- If the deceased has two or more daughters: She is **blocked** (unless a male cousin at her level or below makes her residuary).

### Daughter's Children

Children through daughters do not inherit in classical Islamic law because they are classified as distant kindred (Dhawu al-Arham), not fixed-share or residuary heirs.

## Practical Examples

### Case 1: Grandson Inherits

A man dies leaving: wife, grandson (from a previously deceased son), and a daughter.

- Wife: 1/8
- Daughter: 1/2
- Grandson: remainder as residuary

The grandson inherits because no living son blocks him.

### Case 2: Grandson Blocked

A man dies leaving: wife, a living son, and a grandson (from another deceased son).

- Wife: 1/8
- Son: remainder as residuary
- **Grandson: blocked by the living son — inherits nothing**

This is the painful scenario people commonly ask about.

## The Obligatory Bequest (Wasiyya Wajiba) — A Legal Solution

To address the hardship of grandchildren being blocked by their uncles, many Arab countries have enacted the **obligatory bequest**:

**The concept**: Grandchildren who are denied inheritance because their parent predeceased the grandparent receive a share equivalent to what their parent would have received, up to one-third of the estate.

**Countries that apply it**: Egypt (since 1946), Syria, Jordan, Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, Kuwait, and others — with varying details.

**Important note**: The obligatory bequest is not from classical jurisprudence but a modern legal innovation. Some scholars support it while others oppose it. However, it is legally enforced in many countries.

## Practical Advice

If you're concerned about your grandchildren being excluded:

- **Leave a bequest**: A bequest to non-heirs up to one-third is permissible and recommended.
- **Give during your lifetime**: Gifts given while alive are more reliable.
- **Know your country's law**: Check whether your country applies the obligatory bequest.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My grandchild through my daughter — will they inherit from me?**
Under classical law, no, as they are distant kindred. But you can leave them a bequest of up to one-third.

**My son passed away leaving young children — how do I protect them?**
Leave a bequest of up to one-third, or give them gifts during your lifetime. Check your country's obligatory bequest laws.

**Does a grandson inherit exactly like a son?**
He takes the son's place when no son exists, but he doesn't have the same blocking power. A grandson holds a weaker position than a son in the blocking hierarchy.
